# What I Would Do With Zero Clients

Starting from scratch in Charleston, SC and Spain. No portfolio of paying clients. Here is the exact order I would do things.

---

## Week 1-2: Fix the foundation

### Get the email working
- Set up luis@iberiatechsolutions.com on Namecheap (already done)
- Set up a professional email signature with name, phone, site link
- Forward it to Gmail so you never miss anything

### Set up Stripe
- Create a Stripe account
- Set up 3 subscription products: $149/mo, $249/mo, $449/mo
- Add a simple checkout link you can send to anyone
- This makes you look real. "Here is the link to subscribe" beats "send me a check"

### Set up a request board
- Create a free Trello board with columns: Backlog, In Progress, Review, Done
- This is how clients submit work after they subscribe
- Screenshot it for your site later as proof of process

## Week 3-4: Get your first client for free (or almost free)

This is the hardest part. Nobody wants to be the first.

### Pick 3 local businesses in Charleston with bad websites
- Walk down King Street or drive through West Ashley
- Find restaurants, barber shops, law offices, contractors with sites that look like 2010
- Or businesses with NO website at all (just a Facebook page)

### Build one of them a site for free
- Not "I will build you a site for free if you let me"
- Actually build it. Spend a weekend. Put it on a staging URL
- Then walk in and say: "I built this for you. If you like it, it is $149/mo. If not, no hard feelings."
- This works because they can SEE IT. No risk for them. No pitch. Just results.

### The deal
- Free build, $149/mo starting month 2
- In exchange: a Google review, a testimonial, and permission to use their site in your portfolio
- You lose maybe 15 hours of work. You gain a real client, a real testimonial, and a real case study.

### Do this for 2-3 businesses
- One restaurant or cafe
- One contractor or service business (plumber, electrician, landscaper)
- One professional service (lawyer, accountant, real estate agent)
- Now you have 3 live sites, 3 testimonials, and $450-750/mo recurring

## Month 2-3: Charleston ground game

### Google Business Profile
- Set up IberiaTech Solutions on Google Business
- Address: your Charleston address
- Category: Web Designer, Web Developer
- Post weekly (photos of your work, tips, before/after)
- Ask every client for a Google review

### Local networking (free)
- Charleston Digital Corridor: free coworking, events, startup community
- Charleston Chamber of Commerce: worth the membership if you can afford it
- Show up to any small business meetup or networking event
- Do not pitch. Just talk to people. "I help small businesses get found online" is enough.
- Carry a simple business card with your name, phone, and site

### Cold walk-ins (this actually works in Charleston)
- Charleston is a small-town-big-city. People are friendly.
- Walk into businesses with bad websites. Introduce yourself.
- "I noticed your website could use some love. I help local businesses with that. Here is my card."
- Do NOT be pushy. Leave the card. Follow up in a week.
- Aim for 5-10 walk-ins per week

### Partner with adjacent services
- Find local SEO agencies, marketing consultants, graphic designers
- They get asked "can you build me a website?" all the time
- Offer them a referral fee ($100 or a free month for every client they send)
- Check who is advertising web services on Google in Charleston. Some of them outsource the dev.

## Month 2-3: Spain ground game

### Your advantage in Spain
- You speak native Spanish
- You understand the culture
- You are based in the US (this is prestigious for some Spanish businesses)
- You can serve businesses that want to reach US customers (huge niche)

### Target: Spanish businesses that want US customers
- Restaurants in Spain with tourists (they need English sites)
- Export companies (olive oil, wine, ceramics)
- Tourism businesses (hotels, tours, Airbnb managers)
- Real estate agents selling to American buyers
- These businesses NEED bilingual sites and are underserved

### How to reach them
- LinkedIn: search for business owners in Malaga, Sevilla, Madrid, Barcelona
- Message in Spanish. Be direct: "I help Spanish businesses reach American customers with bilingual websites."
- Instagram: many Spanish small businesses are active here
- Comment genuinely on their posts for a few weeks before pitching
- Facebook groups for Spanish entrepreneurs (emprendedores)

### Directories and listings
- List on Spanish freelance platforms (InfoJobs, Domestika, Fiverr in Spanish)
- Register on Google Business in Spain if possible (remote)
- Join AMETIC or local business chambers

## Month 3-6: Content that brings clients to you

### Start posting on LinkedIn (both markets)
- Post 3x per week minimum
- What to post:
  - Before/after of a client site (anonymize if needed)
  - "I just rebuilt this restaurant's site in 10 days. Here's what changed."
  - Tips for business owners: "3 things your website is probably missing"
  - Your story: "I grew up in Spain and live in Charleston. Here is why I started this."
- Do NOT post generic "5 tips for SEO" stuff. Be personal. Be specific.

### Start a blog on your site (you already have the infrastructure)
- Write 1 post per month minimum
- Target local keywords:
  - "web design charleston sc"
  - "bilingual website designer"
  - "diseño web para empresas en españa"
  - "website for restaurants in charleston"
- Each post should answer a question a business owner would Google

### Before/after content
- Every client site you build, screenshot the old one (or the "no website" state)
- Create a simple before/after image
- This is your best marketing material. Real proof beats any pitch.

## How to close when someone is interested

### The conversation
1. They say they need a website (or you approach them)
2. You say: "Let me show you what I can do. I will build a mockup of your homepage for free. Takes me a couple days."
3. Build the mockup. Send it.
4. If they like it: "Great. It is $249/mo, everything included. I will have the full site live in 2-3 weeks."
5. Send the Stripe link.

### What NOT to do
- Do not send long proposals
- Do not have 3 meetings before starting
- Do not negotiate on price (the price is the price)
- Do not offer discounts (offer more value instead: "I will add a blog section")

### Handle objections
- "That seems expensive" - "A full-time developer costs $10K/mo. I am $249. And you can cancel after a year."
- "I need to think about it" - "No problem. The mockup is yours to keep either way."
- "Can you do it cheaper?" - "The Starter plan is $149/mo if you need fewer pages."
- "What if I do not like it?" - "Full refund before launch. No risk."

## Money math

### Break-even target: 10 clients
| Clients | Monthly revenue | Annual revenue |
|---------|----------------|----------------|
| 3       | $450-750       | $5,400-9,000   |
| 5       | $750-1,250     | $9,000-15,000  |
| 10      | $1,500-2,500   | $18,000-30,000 |
| 20      | $3,000-5,000   | $36,000-60,000 |

### Your costs per client
- Domain: ~$12/yr ($1/mo)
- Hosting: $0 (Vercel free tier for most sites)
- Your time: ~15-20 hours for initial build, ~1-2 hours/mo after
- Total cost per client: roughly $1/mo after the build

### When it gets real
- At 10 clients you have a stable base income
- At 20 clients you are making $3-5K/mo with maybe 30-40 hours/mo of ongoing work
- That is a real business

## What NOT to waste time on

- Perfecting your own website (it is good enough, ship it)
- Learning new frameworks or tools (you know enough, build with what you have)
- Designing logos or business cards for weeks
- Paid ads (you do not have enough margin yet)
- Social media on every platform (pick LinkedIn + one other, that is it)
- Building a CRM or complex systems (a spreadsheet works until you have 15+ clients)
- Waiting for clients to come to you (they will not, you have to go to them first)

## The mindset

You are not selling websites. You are walking into a business and saying:

"I can help you get more customers online. It costs $149 a month. I handle everything. You do not have to think about it."

That is it. That is the pitch. Everything else is details.
